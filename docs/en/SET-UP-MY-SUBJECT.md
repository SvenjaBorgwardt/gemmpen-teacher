# Setting Up My Subject

This guide explains the setup wizard step by step. You can create one configuration per subject or task type. The wizard has six steps, and you move to the next one only when you are ready. There are no technical forms to fill in and nothing to type as code: everything runs through ordinary input fields.

Click "Set up" in the navigation to begin.

## Step 1: Subject and framework

Fill in:

- **Subject**: for example English, German, Business Studies.
- **Language of the student texts** and **language of the feedback**: usually the same, but they can differ.
- **Class level**: for example "Class 11A".
- **Level**: for example "B1-B2".

These details make sure the marking is written in the right language and pitched at the right level.

## Step 2: Task description

Describe what the task is about: what students are meant to write, what they should refer to. You can type it directly or upload a file.

Plain text files (`.txt`, `.md`) are taken over directly. If you upload a PDF or a photo instead, only a placeholder note is put in the field. In that case, type a short note yourself about what the task covers, so the next step (the marking scheme suggestion) fits. The actual content of the file is only read later, during marking itself.

## Step 3: Expected content and marking scheme

This is the most important step. Here you define what the marking looks for to judge whether a piece of work is good.

### What good expected content looks like

Enter one point per line. Each point should be specific and checkable, not vague. A couple of examples:

- Good: "Names at least one concrete example from the text for the described cyberattack."
- Less useful: "Shows understanding of the topic."

Specific expected content helps the marking recognise more precisely what is present in a piece of work and what still needs attention. Vague expected content leads to vaguer reasoning.

You can also upload the expected content as a text file instead of typing it.

### Generating a marking scheme suggestion

Click "Generate scheme suggestion". The marking helper then proposes criteria: a name, a short description, a maximum point value, and, where it makes sense, an all-or-nothing rule (for example: an argument only counts if a claim, a reason, and an example are all three present, otherwise it earns zero points).

This suggestion is a starting point, not a finished result. Afterwards you can:

- Rename criteria, adjust their description, change the point value
- Remove criteria or add new ones by hand
- Turn the all-or-nothing rule on or off and fill in its parts

If the marking helper is not reachable at that moment, you get a test suggestion instead, so you can keep working. A note about this appears right on the screen.

You can also start from one of the built-in sample configurations (English Comment, German Discussion Essay, Business Case Analysis) and adjust it from there.

## Step 4: Grading system and feedback style

Choose:

- **Grading system**: NRW points (0 to 15), grades (1 to 6), or percent.
- **Tone of the feedback**: for example "warm and clear, encouraging".
- **Length**: short, medium, or detailed.
- **With or without a practice suggestion**: adds a short, concrete practice suggestion at the end.

## Step 5: Sample answers and calibration (not required)

This step is optional, but strongly recommended. Enter up to two pieces of work you have already marked yourself: the text of the work and your own points for each criterion.

### Why this matters

The marking helper does not know your own marking habits on its own. With one or two sample answers, you can see in advance how close the marking comes to your own judgement, before you use it on a whole class. We call this calibration.

Click "Start calibration". For each criterion you then see a point difference, together with a plain-language explanation:

- **Close**: the marking is close to your own judgement.
- **Noticeable**: the difference is noticeable; a look at the criterion's descriptors might help.
- **Large**: the difference is large. Check the descriptors and the all-or-nothing rule for this criterion again.

If one criterion shows a large difference, it is worth going back to step 3 and writing a more precise description for it. The more specific a criterion's description, the closer the marking tends to land to your own judgement.

If the marking helper is not reachable, calibration runs as a test so you can still see how it works. A note appears on the screen.

## Step 6: Summary and saving

Give the configuration a display name (for example "English Comment, Class 11A") so you can find it again in the subjects list. You see a summary of everything you entered. Check it, then click "Save configuration".

If anything required is still missing, the page shows you a readable list of what needs attention. After saving, you can go straight to "My subjects" or straight to uploading work for this subject.

## Changing a configuration later

On the "Subjects" page you see all your configurations. For each one you can:

- **Edit**: the wizard opens again with everything you entered before; saving overwrites the existing configuration.
- **Duplicate**: a copy with a new name is created, the original configuration stays untouched.
- **Delete**: with a confirmation step; after that the configuration is gone.
